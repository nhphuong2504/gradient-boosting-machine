# Analysis

This writeup connects the experiments in
[experiments/run_regression_experiments.py](experiments/run_regression_experiments.py)
back to the algorithmic claims in Friedman (2001), and explains what
worked, what did not, and what the results reveal about the method.

All numbers below come from
[experiments/results/summary.txt](experiments/results/summary.txt) and the
PNGs in the same folder, which are regenerated by running the experiments
script.

## What was implemented

Three regression TreeBoost variants from the paper, all driven by the same
stagewise loop in
[src/treeboost/model.py](src/treeboost/model.py):

- **Algorithm 2 — LS Boost.** The pseudo-response is `y - F_{m-1}(x)`, and
  the per-leaf update is just the leaf mean of those residuals. So for LS
  the boosting driver does not need to overwrite the raw least-squares leaf
  values produced by the tree — they are already the optimal `gamma_jm`.
- **Algorithm 3 — LAD TreeBoost.** The tree is fit to `sign(y - F_{m-1})`,
  and `gamma_jm` becomes the median of the residuals inside each leaf. The
  initial constant is the median of `y`. This is the moment where the
  loss-specific leaf-update step in equation (18) actually matters: the
  tree is built on a least-squares criterion, but each leaf's value is
  replaced by a median.
- **Algorithm 4 — M TreeBoost (Huber).** Two extra moving parts: an
  adaptive `delta_m` set to the alpha-quantile of `|r_i|` at the start of
  each iteration (`HuberLoss.update_state`), and the one-step
  Newton-style leaf update around the residual median that the paper
  derives explicitly. The pseudo-response is the residual when
  `|r| <= delta`, clipped to `delta * sign(r)` otherwise.

Every loss-specific behavior is encapsulated behind a small `Loss`
interface, so the boosting loop itself is identical across variants.

## Convergence on a clean signal

Setup (`make_clean_split`): a Friedman-#1-style target

```
f(x) = 10 sin(pi x0 x1) + 20 (x2 - 0.5)^2 + 10 x3 + 5 x4
```

with five uniform features in `[0, 1]`, `n_train = n_test = 600`, and
Gaussian noise `sigma = 1.0`. All three losses use `n_estimators = 200`,
`learning_rate = 0.1`, `max_leaves = 8`.

`experiments/results/summary.txt` shows:

| Loss   | test MSE | test MAE |
|--------|----------|----------|
| LS     | 1.989    | 1.136    |
| LAD    | 2.309    | 1.194    |
| Huber  | 1.977    | 1.124    |

Observations from `convergence_clean.png`:

- All three variants drive their own training loss down monotonically and
  smoothly. The validation curves track the training curves closely after
  ~50 iterations, indicating that the chosen tree depth (`J = 8`) and
  shrinkage (`nu = 0.1`) are not overfitting on this dataset.
- LS and Huber are essentially tied on the clean target, both on MSE and
  MAE. This matches the paper's claim that under approximately Gaussian
  noise Huber should behave like LS.
- LAD pays a small efficiency price under Gaussian noise: its MSE is ~16%
  larger than LS, even though MAE is comparable. That is exactly the
  classic LAD-vs-LS efficiency story — LAD ignores how *far* a residual is,
  so it converges more slowly when the noise is symmetric and light-tailed.

So Algorithm 2 reproduces the textbook L2 behavior, Algorithm 3 reproduces
the slightly less efficient L1 behavior, and Algorithm 4 reproduces the
"basically as good as LS when there are no outliers" behavior the paper
predicts. The implementation passes the "reality check" (Friedman's term
for LS Boost).

## Robustness under heavy-tailed contamination

Setup (`make_contaminated_split`): same target, but 10% of training labels
are perturbed by additive heavy-tailed shocks (`signs * (25 + 12.5 * t_3)`),
producing exactly the kind of mixture distribution Huber and LAD are
designed for. The test set is **clean**: we score predictions against the
true `f(x)`, not against contaminated labels. This is the correct way to
ask "did the method recover the underlying signal?".

`experiments/results/summary.txt`:

| Loss   | clean-MSE | clean-MAE |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| LS     | 33.04     | 3.63      |
| LAD    | 3.49      | 1.17      |
| Huber  | 11.28     | 1.84      |

Several interesting things come out of this number:

- **LS collapses.** Its clean-MSE balloons from ~2.0 (clean training) to
  ~33 (contaminated training). The squared-error loss is dominated by the
  outliers, so each weak learner spends much of its capacity chasing
  ±25 spikes. This is exactly Friedman's motivation in Section 4.
- **LAD recovers cleanly.** Clean-MSE of 3.49 is barely worse than LS
  trained on *clean* data, meaning the method effectively ignored the
  outliers. The reason is mechanical: the negative gradient is `sign(r)`,
  so an outlier residual of 25 contributes the same ±1 as a residual of
  0.5, and the leaf updates are residual *medians*, which are unaffected
  by a small fraction of extreme values.
- **Huber is more robust than LS but less so than LAD here.** On this
  particular contamination — symmetric, very heavy-tailed (`t_3` shocks
  scaled to 25) — Huber's clean-MSE is 11.3, well below LS's 33 but well
  above LAD's 3.5. The paper warns that this depends on the choice of the
  transition point `delta`. With `quantile = 0.85`, roughly the top 15% of
  absolute residuals are treated as outliers; that is enough to keep LS
  behavior on the inliers (good) but still leaves the inflated residuals
  with a non-zero gradient `delta * sign(r)` on each iteration, which
  slowly pulls the model toward outliers across many iterations. LAD's
  pure-sign treatment is more aggressive at the cost of some efficiency on
  the inliers, but on this contamination level the aggressiveness wins.

The bar chart `robustness_bar.png` and the scatter plots
`robustness_true_vs_pred.png` show this visually: LS predictions show
strong deviations from the diagonal `predicted == true` line, while LAD
and Huber stay closely on the diagonal. So the implementation reproduces
the qualitative robustness ordering from the paper (LS << Huber, LAD), and
is honest about the fact that Huber's exact ranking against LAD depends on
how heavy the contamination is and how `delta` is chosen.

## Effect of the learning rate (shrinkage)

Setup: LS Boost on the clean Friedman-#1 split with `max_leaves = 6` and
`n_estimators = 400`, sweeping `nu in {1.0, 0.5, 0.1, 0.05}`.

`shrinkage_curves.png` shows that:

- `nu = 1.0` reaches the floor of the validation MSE in ~30 iterations and
  is then essentially flat (already overfit relative to the tree
  capacity).
- `nu = 0.1` reaches a modestly *lower* validation MSE, but takes ~150-200
  iterations to do so.
- `nu = 0.05` is the slowest and benefits least within the budget of 400
  iterations, illustrating that there is a real trade-off between
  shrinkage and `M`. This matches the paper's Section 5 observation that
  smaller shrinkage requires proportionally more iterations.

This experiment is small but reproduces the standard recommendation that
emerged out of the paper — use moderate shrinkage (`nu ~ 0.05–0.1`) with a
correspondingly larger `M`.

## What worked

- **The `Loss` interface mirrors the paper directly.** Each algorithm
  collapses into three small functions (`initial_prediction`,
  `negative_gradient`, `leaf_update`), and the boosting driver in
  `model.py` is the same for all three. That made it possible to verify
  each piece with focused unit tests rather than only with end-to-end
  training behavior.
- **Best-first tree growth.** Growing the tree by repeatedly expanding the
  highest-gain leaf gives a clean, deterministic way to honor `J = max_leaves`
  in the same sense as the paper, without resorting to depth-first growth
  with awkward extra checks.
- **Tests catch real bugs early.** The unit tests for the loss formulas
  (e.g. `test_huber_leaf_update_matches_paper_formula`,
  `test_lad_leaf_update_is_residual_median`) check exactly the equations
  from Section 4 of the paper. Combined with the integration tests that
  assert "robust loss beats LS under contamination," they protect against
  the most likely silent regressions.

## What did not work / honest limitations

- **The CART splitter is correct but slow.** Each split search sorts the
  feature column inside the current node; for very large `N` and `M` this
  costs more than a global pre-sorted index. The implementation prioritizes
  faithfulness and readability over raw speed, so it is fine for the
  experiments here (a few hundred samples, a few hundred trees) but would
  be a poor base for industrial-scale boosting.
- **No subsampling / no influence trimming.** The paper proposes both
  stochastic gradient boosting and influence trimming for classification
  to speed things up; neither is implemented. The scope was confirmed up
  front to be regression TreeBoost only, so these are deliberate
  omissions, not bugs.
- **Huber ranking depends strongly on `delta`.** The robustness experiment
  showed that with very heavy tails (`t_3` shocks scaled to 25) and a
  default `quantile = 0.85`, Huber lost to LAD on clean MSE. In a more
  thorough study one would either tune `quantile` or report a small grid;
  doing so is straightforward via the `huber_quantile` argument but is not
  done automatically.
- **Mid-iteration LAD loss is non-monotone.** This is expected — the LAD
  pseudo-response is `sign(r)`, which discards magnitude, so the per-step
  greedy choice is not guaranteed to decrease the empirical mean absolute
  error at every iteration even though the overall trend is strongly
  downward. The tests reflect this with a "loss at the end is at most half
  the starting loss" assertion rather than strict monotonicity.

## What the results reveal

- **Friedman's framing as gradient descent in function space is more than
  presentation.** Once `Loss` exposes only `initial_prediction`,
  `negative_gradient`, and `leaf_update`, the boosting loop in `model.py`
  is genuinely loss-agnostic. Adding a fourth regression loss (e.g. the
  quantile loss for quantile regression) would be a few lines.
- **The terminal-region update step is the practical heart of TreeBoost.**
  Without `leaf_update`, the LAD variant would still fit signs and would
  degenerate; with it, the leaf values are residual medians and the method
  is robust. This is the difference between "fit a regression tree to the
  pseudo-residuals" and "fit a regression tree, then re-solve a 1-D
  problem inside each leaf with the original loss." The latter is the
  whole point.
- **Robustness comes at a small efficiency cost on clean data.** The
  clean-data tables show LAD ~16% worse than LS on MSE; the contaminated
  tables show LS ~10x worse than LAD. So a practical recommendation
  consistent with the paper is: prefer Huber when nothing is known about
  contamination (it pays almost nothing on clean data), and tune the
  Huber quantile downward (or fall back to LAD) when contamination is
  known to be substantial.
- **Shrinkage is essentially free regularization.** The shrinkage curves
  reproduce the now-standard finding that smaller `nu` plus larger `M`
  generalizes a little better than `nu = 1`, at the cost of compute. This
  is one of the most robust empirical lessons from the paper, and it
  shows up cleanly in our small experiment.
